Fraud Detection in Financial Transactions Using Machine Learning

An Analysis of the PaySim Synthetic Financial Dataset

Authors:

Lee Hickey

Emma Reen

Sarah O'Hanlon

Abstract

Financial fraud poses a significant threat to the integrity of digital payment systems, resulting in substantial economic losses worldwide. This study investigates the application of machine learning techniques for detecting fraudulent transactions in mobile money systems. Using the PaySim synthetic financial dataset, which simulates real-world mobile money transactions, we develop and evaluate classification models capable of accurately identifying fraudulent activities. The dataset contains over 6.3 million transactions across five transaction types (CASH_IN, CASH_OUT, DEBIT, PAYMENT, and TRANSFER), with approximately 8,213 labeled fraudulent cases representing a highly imbalanced classification problem. Our research explores various approaches to handle class imbalance, feature engineering strategies based on transaction patterns and account behaviors, and the comparative performance of different machine learning algorithms for fraud detection. Results demonstrate that tree-based ensemble methods, particularly XGBoost with SMOTE oversampling, achieve superior performance in detecting fraudulent transactions while maintaining acceptable precision levels.

1. Introduction

The rapid digitalization of financial services has revolutionized how individuals and businesses conduct monetary transactions. Mobile money platforms, digital wallets, and online banking systems have become integral components of the global financial ecosystem, enabling seamless peer-to-peer transfers, merchant payments, and cross-border transactions. While these technological advancements have significantly improved financial inclusion and convenience, they have simultaneously created new opportunities for fraudulent activities. Financial fraud in digital payment systems represents one of the most pressing challenges facing the financial technology sector, with global losses estimated to exceed billions of dollars annually.

The nature of financial fraud has evolved considerably with the advent of digital payment systems. Traditional fraud detection methods, which relied heavily on rule-based systems and manual review processes, have proven inadequate in addressing the sophisticated tactics employed by modern fraudsters. These legacy systems typically operate on predefined thresholds and patterns, making them vulnerable to novel attack vectors and adaptive fraud schemes. Furthermore, the sheer volume of transactions processed by contemporary payment platforms renders manual review impractical, necessitating automated detection mechanisms capable of analyzing millions of transactions in real-time.

Machine learning has emerged as a promising approach for addressing the limitations of traditional fraud detection systems. By learning complex patterns from historical transaction data, machine learning algorithms can identify subtle indicators of fraudulent behavior that may escape rule-based detection mechanisms. These algorithms can adapt to evolving fraud patterns, continuously improving their detection capabilities as new data becomes available. However, the application of machine learning to fraud detection presents unique challenges, most notably the extreme class imbalance inherent in fraud datasets, where legitimate transactions vastly outnumber fraudulent ones.

The class imbalance problem is particularly pronounced in financial fraud detection, where fraudulent transactions typically constitute less than 1% of total transaction volume. This imbalance poses significant challenges for machine learning algorithms, which tend to optimize for overall accuracy rather than minority class detection. A classifier that simply predicts all transactions as legitimate would achieve high accuracy while failing to detect any fraudulent activity, rendering it useless for practical fraud detection applications. Addressing this challenge requires careful consideration of evaluation metrics, sampling strategies, and algorithmic modifications that prioritize the detection of rare but critical fraudulent events.

This study investigates the application of machine learning techniques for fraud detection using the PaySim synthetic financial dataset. PaySim is a financial simulator that generates synthetic transaction data based on patterns observed in real-world mobile money logs from an African mobile money service provider. The synthetic nature of the dataset addresses privacy concerns associated with real financial data while preserving the statistical properties and fraud patterns characteristic of actual transaction logs. The dataset comprises over 6.3 million transactions across five transaction types, with approximately 8,213 labeled fraudulent cases representing a fraud rate of approximately 0.13%.

Our research addresses three fundamental questions that are critical for developing effective fraud detection systems. First, we investigate which machine learning algorithms perform best for detecting fraud in highly imbalanced financial transaction data. This involves comparing traditional algorithms such as logistic regression with more sophisticated ensemble methods including random forests, gradient boosting, and XGBoost. Second, we examine which transaction features are most predictive of fraudulent behavior, employing feature importance analysis and correlation studies to identify key indicators of fraud. Third, we explore how class imbalance can be effectively addressed to minimize false negatives (missed fraud) while maintaining acceptable precision levels, comparing various resampling techniques and algorithmic modifications.

The contributions of this study are threefold. We provide a comprehensive comparative analysis of machine learning algorithms for fraud detection, evaluating performance across multiple metrics relevant to imbalanced classification problems. We identify and validate key features that distinguish fraudulent from legitimate transactions, informing feature engineering strategies for future fraud detection systems. Finally, we assess the effectiveness of various class imbalance handling techniques, providing practical guidance for practitioners developing fraud detection models.

2. Related Work

The application of machine learning to financial fraud detection has been extensively studied in the academic literature, with researchers exploring various algorithmic approaches, feature engineering strategies, and techniques for handling class imbalance. This section reviews relevant prior work, situating our study within the broader context of fraud detection research.

2.1 Traditional Approaches to Fraud Detection

Early fraud detection systems relied primarily on rule-based approaches, employing expert-defined thresholds and patterns to identify suspicious transactions. Bolton and Hand (2002) provided a comprehensive overview of statistical fraud detection methods, highlighting the limitations of rule-based systems in adapting to evolving fraud patterns. These systems typically suffer from high false positive rates and require continuous manual updating to remain effective against new fraud tactics. Despite these limitations, rule-based systems remain in use due to their interpretability and regulatory compliance advantages.

The transition from rule-based to data-driven approaches began with the application of classical statistical methods to fraud detection. Logistic regression became a popular choice for fraud classification due to its probabilistic interpretation and computational efficiency. Maes et al. (2002) demonstrated the effectiveness of logistic regression for credit card fraud detection, achieving reasonable detection rates while maintaining model interpretability. However, these linear models struggle to capture the complex, non-linear relationships often present in fraudulent transaction patterns.

2.2 Machine Learning for Fraud Detection

The advent of machine learning brought more sophisticated approaches to fraud detection. Decision trees and their ensemble variants have proven particularly effective for fraud classification. Bhattacharyya et al. (2011) conducted a comprehensive comparison of machine learning techniques for credit card fraud detection, finding that random forests outperformed individual decision trees, neural networks, and logistic regression across multiple evaluation metrics. The ensemble nature of random forests provides robustness against overfitting while capturing complex feature interactions.

Gradient boosting methods have emerged as state-of-the-art approaches for tabular data classification, including fraud detection. Chen and Guestrin (2016) introduced XGBoost, a scalable implementation of gradient boosting that has achieved exceptional performance across numerous machine learning competitions and real-world applications. Alwadain et al. (2023) demonstrated the superiority of XGBoost for financial fraud detection, attributing its success to its ability to handle missing values, capture non-linear relationships, and provide regularization against overfitting.

Deep learning approaches have also been explored for fraud detection, though their advantages over traditional machine learning methods for tabular financial data remain debated. Fiore et al. (2019) proposed a generative adversarial network (GAN) approach for generating synthetic fraud samples, addressing the class imbalance problem while improving detection performance. However, the computational requirements and reduced interpretability of deep learning models have limited their adoption in production fraud detection systems where model explainability is often a regulatory requirement.

2.3 Handling Class Imbalance

The extreme class imbalance characteristic of fraud datasets has motivated extensive research into resampling and algorithmic techniques for improving minority class detection. Chawla et al. (2002) introduced SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique), which generates synthetic minority class samples by interpolating between existing minority instances. SMOTE and its variants have become widely adopted in fraud detection applications, consistently improving recall for fraudulent transactions.

Undersampling techniques, which reduce the majority class to balance the dataset, offer an alternative approach to handling imbalance. Liu et al. (2009) proposed EasyEnsemble, combining undersampling with ensemble learning to leverage the full majority class while training balanced classifiers. Random undersampling, despite its simplicity, has shown competitive performance in fraud detection applications when combined with appropriate classification algorithms.

Cost-sensitive learning provides an algorithmic approach to handling imbalance by assigning higher misclassification costs to minority class errors. Elkan (2001) established the theoretical foundations of cost-sensitive learning, demonstrating its equivalence to resampling under certain conditions. Many modern machine learning implementations, including XGBoost, provide built-in support for class weighting, enabling straightforward application of cost-sensitive approaches.

2.4 Feature Engineering for Fraud Detection

The quality of features used for fraud detection significantly impacts model performance. Domain knowledge plays a crucial role in identifying predictive features that distinguish fraudulent from legitimate transactions. Jha et al. (2012) proposed transaction aggregation features, computing statistics over historical transactions associated with each account to capture behavioral patterns indicative of fraud. These aggregated features, such as transaction frequency, average amount, and deviation from typical behavior, have proven highly predictive.

Bahnsen et al. (2016) introduced cost-sensitive feature selection for fraud detection, demonstrating that traditional feature selection methods may not optimize for the business objectives of fraud detection. Their work highlighted the importance of considering the financial impact of fraud when designing feature sets and evaluation metrics.

2.5 The PaySim Dataset

The PaySim dataset, introduced by Lopez-Rojas et al. (2016), addresses the scarcity of publicly available fraud detection datasets by providing synthetic data that preserves the statistical properties of real mobile money transactions. The simulator was calibrated using one month of financial logs from an African mobile money provider, ensuring realistic transaction patterns and fraud scenarios. The synthetic nature of the data enables unrestricted research use while protecting sensitive customer information.

Several studies have utilized the PaySim dataset for fraud detection research. Scarff (2018) demonstrated the effectiveness of temporal modeling approaches, highlighting the importance of considering the sequential nature of transactions when training fraud detection models. Bontempi (2018) explored the challenges of temporal validation in fraud detection, advocating for time-based train-test splits that better reflect production deployment scenarios.

3. Methodology

3.1 Dataset Description

This study utilizes the PaySim synthetic financial dataset, comprising 6,362,620 transactions simulating one month of mobile money activity. The dataset includes five transaction types: CASH_IN (deposits), CASH_OUT (withdrawals), DEBIT (direct debits), PAYMENT (merchant payments), and TRANSFER (peer-to-peer transfers). Each transaction record contains eleven features: step (hourly time unit), type (transaction category), amount (transaction value), nameOrig (originating account identifier), oldbalanceOrg and newbalanceOrig (account balance before and after transaction), nameDest (destination account identifier), oldbalanceDest and newbalanceDest (destination balance before and after), isFraud (target variable), and isFlaggedFraud (system-generated fraud flag).

The target variable isFraud indicates fraudulent transactions, with 8,213 positive cases (0.13%) representing the minority class. This extreme imbalance reflects real-world fraud rates and presents a significant classification challenge. Preliminary analysis revealed that fraudulent transactions occur exclusively in TRANSFER and CASH_OUT types, providing domain knowledge that informs feature engineering.

3.2 Feature Engineering

We developed engineered features capturing transaction patterns and account behaviors indicative of fraud. Balance change features compute the difference between pre- and post-transaction balances for both originating and destination accounts. Error flags identify discrepancies between expected and actual balance changes, which may indicate fraudulent manipulation. Zero balance indicators flag transactions resulting in complete account drainage, a common fraud pattern. Amount-to-balance ratios normalize transaction amounts relative to account balances, capturing unusually large transactions. A full drainage flag specifically identifies transactions that completely empty an account. Temporal features extract hour-of-day and day-of-simulation from the step variable, enabling detection of time-based fraud patterns.

Categorical encoding transformed the transaction type variable into numeric format using label encoding. The final feature set comprises 18 features, including both original and engineered variables. All features were standardized using z-score normalization to ensure comparable scales for algorithms sensitive to feature magnitudes.

3.3 Experimental Design

Data was partitioned using stratified sampling to preserve class distributions, with 70% allocated to training and 30% to testing. To manage computational requirements while maintaining representative samples, experiments utilized a sample of 500,000 training instances for model comparison.

For Research Question 1, we compared five classification algorithms: Logistic Regression (baseline linear model), Decision Tree (interpretable single-tree classifier), Random Forest (bagging ensemble), Gradient Boosting (sequential ensemble), and XGBoost (optimized gradient boosting). All algorithms were implemented using scikit-learn and XGBoost libraries with default hyperparameters adjusted for the imbalanced classification context.

For Research Question 2, feature importance was extracted from the best-performing model using built-in importance measures. Correlation analysis computed Pearson coefficients between features and the target variable. Distribution comparisons examined feature value differences between fraudulent and legitimate transactions.

For Research Question 3, we evaluated five imbalance handling approaches: baseline (no handling), random undersampling, SMOTE oversampling, class weight adjustment, and a combined approach using undersampling with class weights. All methods were implemented using the imbalanced-learn library integrated with scikit-learn pipelines.

3.4 Evaluation Metrics

Given the class imbalance, we employed multiple evaluation metrics beyond accuracy. ROC-AUC measures discrimination ability across all classification thresholds. Precision quantifies the proportion of predicted frauds that are actually fraudulent. Recall (sensitivity) measures the proportion of actual frauds correctly identified. F1-score provides the harmonic mean of precision and recall. Average Precision summarizes the precision-recall curve. Confusion matrix analysis reveals true positives, false negatives, false positives, and true negatives.

4. Experiments

4.1 Algorithm Comparison (RQ1)

We trained five classification algorithms on the standardized feature set to determine which performs best for fraud detection. Each algorithm was configured within a scikit-learn Pipeline incorporating StandardScaler preprocessing. Logistic Regression used L2 regularization with 1000 maximum iterations. Decision Tree was limited to maximum depth of 10 to prevent overfitting. Random Forest employed 100 trees with maximum depth of 10 and parallel processing. Gradient Boosting used 100 estimators with maximum depth of 4 and learning rate of 0.1. XGBoost matched the Gradient Boosting configuration with additional binary logistic objective and logloss evaluation metric.

Models were trained on the 500,000-instance training sample and evaluated on the full test set. We computed ROC-AUC, accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and average precision for each algorithm. ROC curves and precision-recall curves were generated to visualize performance across classification thresholds.

4.2 Feature Importance Analysis (RQ2)

Using the best-performing algorithm identified in RQ1, we conducted comprehensive feature importance analysis. XGBoost was retrained with increased estimators (200) and depth (6) to improve feature importance estimates, incorporating scale_pos_weight to handle imbalance. Built-in feature importance scores were extracted and ranked.

Correlation analysis computed the Pearson correlation coefficient between each feature and the target variable across the training set. Features were ranked by absolute correlation magnitude to identify both positive and negative fraud indicators. Distribution analysis compared mean feature values between fraudulent and legitimate transaction groups, quantifying the discriminative power of each feature.

4.3 Imbalance Handling Evaluation (RQ3)

Five imbalance handling strategies were implemented using imbalanced-learn pipelines with XGBoost as the base classifier. The baseline approach applied no resampling or weighting. Random undersampling reduced the majority class to match minority class size. SMOTE generated synthetic minority samples using 5 nearest neighbors for interpolation. Class weighting set scale_pos_weight equal to the imbalance ratio (approximately 770:1). The combined approach applied undersampling to achieve a 2:1 ratio followed by class weighting of 2.

All methods were evaluated on the test set using the same metrics as RQ1, with additional analysis of false negatives (missed frauds) and false positives (false alarms). Confusion matrices visualized the error distributions for each method. Threshold analysis examined how varying the classification threshold affects the precision-recall trade-off for the best-performing imbalance handling method.

5. Results

5.1 Algorithm Performance (RQ1)

Experimental results demonstrate clear performance differences among the evaluated algorithms. XGBoost achieved the highest ROC-AUC, confirming its superiority for fraud detection in imbalanced datasets. Random Forest performed comparably, followed by Gradient Boosting, Decision Tree, and Logistic Regression. The performance gap between ensemble methods and single models highlights the importance of combining multiple learners for fraud detection.

Precision-recall analysis revealed that tree-based ensemble methods maintain higher precision at equivalent recall levels compared to linear models. The ROC curves showed that XGBoost and Random Forest achieve near-perfect true positive rates at low false positive rates, indicating strong discriminative power. Logistic Regression's linear decision boundary limits its ability to capture complex fraud patterns, resulting in lower overall performance.

These findings support the hypothesis that non-linear ensemble methods are better suited for fraud detection, where fraudulent patterns often involve complex interactions between multiple features. The superior performance of XGBoost specifically can be attributed to its regularization mechanisms, handling of missing values, and efficient gradient-based optimization.

5.2 Feature Importance (RQ2)

Feature importance analysis identified several highly predictive features for fraud detection. Transaction amount emerged as the most important feature, with fraudulent transactions exhibiting significantly higher average amounts than legitimate ones. Balance change features ranked highly, capturing the distinctive patterns of fraudulent fund transfers. The engineered full_drain flag, identifying complete account drainage, showed strong predictive power despite its simplicity.

Correlation analysis revealed both positive and negative fraud indicators. Features positively correlated with fraud include amount_to_orig_balance (large transactions relative to account balance), orig_zero_balance (transactions leaving zero balance), and full_drain (complete account drainage). Negatively correlated features include newbalanceOrig (higher remaining balance indicates legitimacy) and certain transaction types.

Distribution analysis confirmed that fraudulent transactions cluster in specific regions of the feature space. Fraud cases show distinctively higher transaction amounts, higher amount-to-balance ratios, and higher probability of resulting in zero balance. These patterns align with typical fraud behaviors: draining accounts quickly before detection.

5.3 Imbalance Handling (RQ3)

Imbalance handling experiments revealed significant improvements over the baseline approach. SMOTE achieved the highest recall, successfully identifying a substantially greater proportion of fraudulent transactions compared to the baseline. Class weighting provided comparable recall improvements with lower computational overhead. Undersampling improved recall but sacrificed some precision due to majority class information loss. The combined approach balanced precision and recall effectively.

False negative analysis showed dramatic reductions in missed frauds when employing imbalance handling techniques. The baseline model missed a significant portion of fraudulent transactions, while SMOTE and class weighting reduced false negatives substantially. This reduction in missed frauds directly translates to financial loss prevention in production systems.

Threshold analysis demonstrated that lowering the classification threshold from 0.5 to 0.3 increases recall while maintaining acceptable precision for the weighted XGBoost model. This finding suggests that threshold tuning provides an additional mechanism for optimizing fraud detection performance based on business requirements and acceptable false positive rates.

6. Conclusion and Future Work

This study investigated machine learning approaches for financial fraud detection using the PaySim synthetic dataset. Our experiments addressed three research questions regarding algorithm selection, feature importance, and class imbalance handling.

Regarding algorithm performance, XGBoost and Random Forest demonstrated superior fraud detection capabilities compared to simpler models, achieving higher ROC-AUC, precision, and recall. The non-linear nature and ensemble structure of these algorithms enable effective capture of complex fraud patterns.

Feature analysis revealed that transaction amount, balance changes, and account drainage indicators are the most predictive features for fraud detection. Engineered features based on domain knowledge, such as full_drain and balance error flags, provide substantial discriminative power, validating the importance of thoughtful feature engineering.

Class imbalance handling significantly improves fraud detection performance. SMOTE oversampling and class weighting both achieve substantial reductions in false negatives while maintaining acceptable precision. For production deployment, class weighting offers computational efficiency advantages, while threshold tuning provides additional optimization flexibility.

Future work should explore several promising directions. Real-time detection systems incorporating streaming data processing would enable immediate fraud intervention. Deep learning approaches, particularly recurrent neural networks capturing transaction sequences, may improve detection of sophisticated fraud patterns. Federated learning could enable collaborative fraud detection across financial institutions while preserving data privacy. Finally, explainable AI techniques would enhance model interpretability, facilitating regulatory compliance and fraud investigator trust.

7. Ethics Statement

This research utilizes synthetic data generated by the PaySim simulator, which preserves the statistical properties of real financial transactions without exposing actual customer information. No real personal or financial data was accessed or analyzed during this study. The synthetic nature of the dataset ensures complete privacy protection while enabling meaningful fraud detection research.

We acknowledge the potential dual-use concerns associated with fraud detection research. While our findings aim to improve financial system security and protect consumers from fraudulent losses, the same techniques could theoretically inform adversarial attacks on fraud detection systems. We have refrained from publishing specific evasion strategies or vulnerabilities that could enable fraud.

The deployment of machine learning fraud detection systems raises fairness considerations. Automated systems may inadvertently discriminate against certain demographic groups if training data reflects historical biases. We recommend that practitioners deploying fraud detection models conduct fairness audits and implement bias mitigation strategies to ensure equitable treatment across customer populations.

Finally, we recognize the importance of human oversight in fraud detection decisions. Machine learning models should augment rather than replace human judgment, particularly for high-stakes decisions affecting customer accounts. We advocate for human-in-the-loop systems that combine algorithmic efficiency with human expertise and accountability.
